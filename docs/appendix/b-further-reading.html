Appendix B

Further Reading

This course is a first pass — deep enough to build a real engine, but every part has a rabbit hole. These are the sources this material is built on and the best places to go deeper, grouped by the part they extend. Titles and authors are given so you can find the current edition; a few have stable homes linked directly.

Integration & the timestep (Ch 5)

  • Glenn Fiedler — "Fix Your Timestep!" and "Integration Basics" (gafferongames.com). The clearest explanation of the fixed-timestep accumulator and why semi-implicit Euler beats the naive one — Chapter 5 is essentially a retelling.
  • 3Blue1Brown — "Essence of Linear Algebra" (video series). If the vector/matrix intuition in Parts I felt shaky, this is the gold-standard visual refresher for dot/cross products and what a matrix really does.

Particles, cloth & position-based dynamics (Ch 6–9, 20)

  • Thomas Jakobsen — "Advanced Character Physics" (GDC 2001). The Verlet-plus-constraint-projection method behind our ropes and cloth, from the Hitman engine.
  • Matthias Müller et al. — "Position Based Dynamics" (2007), and Macklin, Müller et al. — "XPBD" (2016). The papers behind Parts II and VII; XPBD adds the compliance term that made our jello's stiffness physical. Müller's "Ten Minute Physics" tutorials are an outstanding, code-first companion.
  • Ian Millington — "Game Physics Engine Development" (book). Builds a particle-and-rigid-body engine from scratch, C++ throughout; the force-generator pattern in Chapter 6 comes straight from here.

Rigid-body dynamics (Ch 10–11)

  • David Baraff & Andrew Witkin — "Physically Based Modeling: Principles and Practice" (SIGGRAPH course notes). Baraff's rigid-body sections derive the inertia tensor, torque, and angular momentum carefully — the theory under Chapter 11.
  • The Dzhanibekov / tennis-racket theorem. Search for the cosmonaut Vladimir Dzhanibekov's wingnut footage from the space station, and the "intermediate axis theorem" — the same effect our T-handle reproduces, and a lovely rabbit hole into Euler's equations.

Collision detection (Ch 12–15)

  • Christer Ericson — "Real-Time Collision Detection" (book, "RTCD"). The definitive reference — AABBs, broadphase, closest-point tests, and the SAT. If you buy one physics book, this is it.
  • Dirk Gregorius — GDC talks on the Separating-Axis Test and robust contact creation. The practical details of box-box SAT and stable manifold generation behind Chapter 14.
  • Casey Muratori — "Implementing GJK" (video), and Gino van den Bergen — "Collision Detection in Interactive 3D Environments" (book). The clearest walkthroughs of GJK. Kevin Moran's GJK/EPA article at winter.dev is the direct model for our implementation — including the consistently-wound EPA polytope that fixed our divergence bug.

Collision response, the solver & joints (Ch 16–19)

  • Erin Catto — GDC talks and Box2D (box2d.org). "Fast and Simple Physics using Sequential Impulses", "Soft Constraints", and "Understanding Constraints" are the foundation of our entire Part V–VI solver — sequential impulses, warm starting, Baumgarte, and the constraint formulation for joints. Box2D itself is a masterclass in readable engine code.
  • Randy Gaul — "Impulse Engine" and blog. Approachable, well-commented implementations of impulse-based response and manifold generation.

Rendering (Ch 4)

  • Akenine-Möller, Haines et al. — "Real-Time Rendering" (book). Far beyond our software rasteriser, but the canonical reference for the transform pipeline, projection, and everything a real GPU renderer does.
  • The SDL3 documentation (wiki.libsdl.org) for the windowing, input, and SDL_RenderGeometry calls the whole project sits on.
How to use this list Don't read it front to back. When a chapter leaves you wanting more — "but how do real engines make stacks truly stable?", "what if the shapes aren't convex?" — come back and pull the matching thread. Everything here assumes exactly the foundation you just built.
