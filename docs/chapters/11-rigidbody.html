Rigid-Body Dynamics

Now we give bodies the rotational half of Newton’s laws: torque, the inertia tensor, and angular momentum. Get them right and something genuinely spooky appears for free — a handle spinning in space that flips itself end-over-end, again and again, with nothing touching it. It’s not a trick; it’s physics, and by the end of this page you’ll know exactly why it happens.

Goal

Understand the inertia tensor and torque, integrate a spinning body stably, and reproduce the Dzhanibekov (intermediate-axis) effect. Code: physics/rigidbody.hpp and physics/inertia.hpp; demo: chapters/11-rigidbody/main.cpp.

Two copies of Newton, side by side

Everything about rotation mirrors something you already know about straight-line motion. Learn the correspondence and the whole chapter clicks into place:

Linear (motion)Angular (rotation)
force Ftorque τ=r×F
mass minertia tensor I
velocity vangular velocity ω
momentum p=mvangular momentum L=Iω
position xorientation q (a quaternion)

The two halves run in parallel every step. A force through the centre of mass just moves the body; the interesting new ingredient is a force applied off-centre.

Torque: off-centre forces spin things

Push a door at its handle and it swings; push right at the hinge and it doesn’t. Same force, different where. That “where” is captured by torque, the cross product of the lever arm r (from the centre of mass to where the force is applied) with the force:

τ=r×F

The inertia tensor: rotation’s version of mass

Mass answers “how hard is this to get moving in a straight line?” The inertia tensor answers “how hard is this to get spinning?” — and the answer depends on the axis. A pencil is trivial to spin about its length but hard to spin end-over-end. Because it differs by axis, a single number won’t do; we need a 3×3 matrix.

What the numbers mean For a body aligned with its own natural axes (its principal axes), the tensor is diagonal — just three numbers, the moments of inertia about x, y, z. Each says how much the mass is spread out away from that axis (mass far from the axis resists spinning more, which is why a figure skater speeds up by pulling their arms in). For a solid box with half-sizes h, the moment about x mixes the other two dimensions: Ixx=m3(hy2+hz2).

The tensor is stored in body space (constant, since a rigid body never changes shape). To use it in the world as the body turns, we rotate it: Iworld=R I RT, where R is the body’s current rotation. Composite bodies (our T-handle = shaft + crossbar) add their parts’ tensors via the parallel-axis theorem — see inertia::shift.

The crucial choice: store angular momentum, not ω

Under no torque, Newton says angular momentum L is conserved — exactly constant. Angular velocity ω is not: as the body turns, the same L corresponds to a different ω. So we store the constant thing, L, and recover ω each step from ω=Iworld−1L.

A Chapter 5 lesson, again — in disguise

Our first attempt turned the orientation with a naive explicit step (q+=12ω⊗q dt). It kept L perfectly, yet the body gained energy and spun faster and faster — the exact energy-leak from Chapter 5, now wearing a rotational costume. The fix is the same in spirit: use a better step. We advance the orientation with an exponential-map midpoint method — sample ω at the middle of the step and rotate by the exact angle |ω| dt. Energy holds steady, and free rotation finally behaves like the real thing.

// ω = I⁻¹_world · L, sampled at the midpoint for stability (see rigidbody.hpp)
const Vec3 omegaStart = invInertiaWorld() * angularMomentum;
const Quat qMid = normalize(spin(omegaStart, dt * 0.5f) * orientation);
const Vec3 omegaMid = invInertiaWorldFor(qMid) * angularMomentum;
orientation = normalize(spin(omegaMid, dt) * orientation);   // exact rotation by |ω|·dt

The payoff: the Dzhanibekov effect

Every body has three principal axes. Spin it about the one with the smallest moment of inertia, or the largest, and it spins serenely. Spin it about the one in the middle and it can’t hold still: the tiniest wobble grows until the body flips completely over — then flips back, over and over. This is the intermediate-axis theorem, made famous by cosmonaut Vladimir Dzhanibekov filming a wingnut doing it on the space station (it’s also called the tennis-racket theorem — toss a racket and try to catch it the same way up).

Why it flips Two quantities are fixed for a free spin: angular momentum L and kinetic energy. Those two constraints carve out a path the spin must follow. For the smallest and largest axes that path is a tiny loop around the axis — stable. For the intermediate axis the path is a huge loop that swings all the way around to the opposite pole and back — so the body turns right over. Our simulation reproduces it only because it conserves both L (exactly) and energy (very nearly) — which is why the integrator above mattered so much.

The HUD shows |L| holding rock-steady while |ω| wanders: that contrast is the effect. Scene 2 spins three handles, one per principal axis — only the middle one misbehaves. Scene 3 fires a thruster off-centre so you can see torque = r×F both push and spin.

Experiments

  • In scene 2, orbit so you’re looking down the row. The left (min-axis) and right (max-axis) handles keep their orientation; only the middle one tumbles. Same object, same speed — only the chosen axis differs.
  • Watch the HUD in scene 1 across a flip. |L| never budges. If you have an older build where energy leaked, you’d instead see |ω| climb without bound — the bug that taught us to use the midpoint step.
  • In scene 3, note it keeps drifting after the thruster stops: no friction here, so momentum (linear and angular) is conserved and it coasts forever.

Takeaways

  • Rotation mirrors linear motion: torque ↔ force, inertia tensor ↔ mass, angular momentum ↔ momentum.
  • Torque τ=r×F: off-centre forces spin a body.
  • Store the conserved quantity L; derive ω=I−1L. Integrate orientation carefully or energy leaks (Chapter 5 rides again).
  • The Dzhanibekov / intermediate-axis flip falls out for free from conserving both L and energy.
