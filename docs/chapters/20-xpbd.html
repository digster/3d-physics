XPBD & Soft Bodies

The finale. Everything until now has been perfectly rigid. Let's make something squish. A soft body is — one last time — a lattice of particles held by distance constraints, exactly like the cloth of Chapter 9. One new idea, compliance, turns those constraints into real springs, and the cube becomes jello.

Goal

Understand XPBD (Extended Position-Based Dynamics) and compliance, and use it to build a jiggling jello cube. Engine: physics/softbody.hpp. Demo: chapters/20-xpbd/main.cpp.

Where Chapter 8 left off

Back in Ropes we satisfied a distance constraint by simply snapping the two particles to the right distance — a perfectly rigid link. If we wanted softness, our only knob was the number of iterations, which is a hack: the "spring" got stiffer or softer depending on how hard the computer was working, not on any physical property. For jello we want a real, tunable stiffness that means the same thing no matter the timestep. That's exactly what XPBD adds.

Compliance: a real spring in one extra term

Intuition XPBD gives every constraint a compliance α — its inverse stiffness (soft = big α, rigid = α=0). Instead of snapping the constraint all the way, it solves it like a spring that's allowed to stay a little bit stretched, storing an accumulated force (a Lagrange multiplier λ) as it goes. The beautiful result: the stiffness you dial in is a genuine material property, independent of the timestep and the iteration count — the thing Chapter 8's approach could never promise.

For a distance constraint C=|p1−p2|−L, each solve computes a change in the multiplier and moves the particles by it:

Δλ= −C−α~λ w1+w2+α~ , α~=αΔt2

where wi are the inverse masses. Then each particle moves along the constraint direction by wi Δλ. Set α=0 and this collapses back to Chapter 8's rigid snap; turn it up and the link softens into a spring. That's the whole difference between a brick and a blob.

Small steps beat big steps

XPBD gets its famous stability from substepping: rather than one big step with many constraint iterations, it takes many tiny steps with just one constraint solve each. Each substep does the familiar dance — predict positions, solve the constraints once, then read the new velocity back from how far things actually moved:

for (int s = 0; s < substeps; ++s) {
    for (auto& p : particles) {                 // predict
        p.prevPosition = p.position;
        p.velocity += gravity * h;
        p.position += p.velocity * h;
    }
    for (auto& c : springs) { c.lambda = 0; solveXPBD(c, h); }   // solve once
    // ...collide with the floor...
    for (auto& p : particles)                    // velocity from actual movement
        p.velocity = (p.position - p.prevPosition) / h;
}

Building a cube that keeps its shape

A grid of particles joined only along the edges would fold flat like a cardboard box. So, exactly as the cloth needed shear links, a solid needs enough constraints to resist every way it could collapse. We weave three families through the lattice:

  • Structural — along the three axes (the edges).
  • Shear — the face diagonals, resisting skew.
  • Volume — the body diagonals across each cell, resisting being squashed flat.

./build/bin/ch20_xpbd           # 1 jello drop · 2 stiff→soft row · 3 hanging jello

Scene 2 is the money shot — four cubes, stiff (green) to soft (coral), dropped together: same fall, wildly different squish, all from the one compliance number. Experiments

  • In the code, push the compliance toward 0.2 and the cube collapses into a puddle and never recovers — too soft to hold itself up. Toward 0 and it's a rigid box again. Jello lives in the sweet spot between.
  • Run scene 2 and watch the green (stiff) cube keep its shape while the coral (soft) one flattens to a fraction of its height — the same drop, the same solver, one parameter apart.

Takeaways

  • A soft body is a lattice of particles + distance constraints — the same idea as cloth.
  • XPBD adds a compliance α and an accumulated multiplier λ, giving a real, timestep-independent stiffness (α=0 is rigid).
  • Substepping — many tiny steps, one solve each — is what makes it stable.
  • Structural + shear + volume constraints keep a solid from collapsing.
The end — you built a physics engine

Twenty chapters ago there was an empty window. Now there's a real, if compact, physics engine, written from nothing: the vector, matrix, and quaternion math; a software 3D renderer; stable numerical integration; particles, springs, ropes, and cloth; rigid bodies that spin correctly, including the eerie Dzhanibekov flip; broadphase, SAT, and GJK to find contacts; an impulse solver with restitution, friction, stacking, and sleeping to resolve them; joints for chains, hinges, and ragdolls; and now soft bodies that squish and spring back.

Every piece was built the hard way, on purpose — because the point was never to use a physics engine, but to understand exactly why each part of one works. From here, every simulation you meet is made of ideas you now own. Thanks for building it all the way through.
