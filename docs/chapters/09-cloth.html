Cloth

Cloth looks complicated and is almost embarrassingly simple: it’s a grid of the Verlet particles from the last chapter, laced together with distance constraints. Choose the right three kinds of link, add a breeze, and you have a flag. This is where Part II pays off.

Goal

Build a waving flag from a grid of particles and three kinds of constraint, and render it as a solid, double-sided sheet. Reuses physics/particle.hpp and physics/constraint.hpp unchanged. Demo: chapters/09-cloth/main.cpp.

Cloth is a grid of beads

Lay particles out on a grid. Number them by row and column. Now the only question is which particles to connect with distance constraints — and that choice is the entire difference between a floppy mess and a convincing fabric. We use three kinds of link, each preventing a specific way the grid would otherwise fail:

  • Structural — each particle to its right and down neighbour. These set the cloth’s size and stop it stretching.
  • Shear — the diagonals of each cell. Without them the grid has no resistance to skewing and collapses flat like a folding lattice gate.
  • Bend — reach past the immediate neighbour to the one two away. These resist sharp folds, so the cloth creases in smooth waves instead of knife-edges.
Intuition Think of the three link types as answering three different “no”s. Structural: “no, you can’t stretch.” Shear: “no, you can’t skew into a flat diamond.” Bend: “no, you can’t fold sharply.” Each is the same humble distance constraint from Chapter 8 — only which particles it joins differs.

Same solver, bigger web

There is no new physics here at all. Every step is exactly the rope’s loop — integrate every particle with Verlet, then relax the whole constraint list a few times — just over hundreds of particles and thousands of links instead of a line of a dozen:

for (auto& p : particles) p.integrateVerlet(gravity + wind, dt);
satisfyConstraints(constraints, particles, iterations);

Pinning is the same trick too: the demo makes the left column (the “pole”) static with invMass = 0, and everything else hangs and flaps from it. Wind is just an extra acceleration added in, gusting over time so the ripple looks alive.

Drawing the sheet

The particles are the simulation; to see a surface we build a triangle mesh from them each frame — two triangles per grid cell, their corners being four neighbouring particles. Because a flag is a thin sheet with no “inside”, we draw it double-sided: we skip backface culling and light whichever face points at the light, so the cloth stays visible and shaded as it twists toward and away from you.

// refresh the mesh vertices from the live particle positions, then draw
for (size_t i = 0; i < particles.size(); ++i)
    clothMesh.positions[i] = particles[i].position;
r.drawMesh(clothMesh, Mat4::identity(), fabricColor, /*doubleSided=*/true);
See the weave Press W in the demo to switch to wireframe — the flag becomes its underlying grid of links, and you can watch the structural, shear, and bend constraints all doing their jobs as it billows.

Run it

./build/bin/ch09_cloth          # 1 flag (pole on left) · 2 curtain · 3 corners + gusts

Scene 2 pins the top edge for a hanging curtain; scene 3 pins only the two top corners and turns the wind way up, so the sheet stretches and snaps between its anchors.

Experiments

  • In the code, comment out the shear links and rerun. The flag loses all resistance to skew and sags into a collapsed, stringy shape — a vivid demonstration of what shear links are for.
  • Then comment out the bend links instead: the cloth still holds its shape but folds sharply, losing that soft draping quality.
  • Lower the constraint iterations and the fabric turns stretchy and rubbery, exactly as the rope did — same solver, same knob.

Takeaways

  • Cloth is a grid of Verlet particles joined by distance constraints — no new physics beyond Chapter 8.
  • Structural / shear / bend links each forbid one failure mode: stretching, skewing, sharp creasing.
  • Rebuild a mesh from the particle positions each frame; draw thin surfaces double-sided.
End of Part II You’ve built a real little soft-body engine: fountains, springs, ropes, and cloth, all from particles, forces, and constraints. Everything so far has been a point, though — nothing has had an orientation or spun.
