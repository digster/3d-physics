Narrowphase II: SAT & Manifolds

Spheres were easy. Two oriented boxes are the first genuinely tricky case — and the Separating-Axis Theorem cracks it with a beautifully simple idea: if two convex shapes are apart, there’s some direction along which their shadows don’t overlap. Find that direction, or prove it doesn’t exist.

Goal

Understand SAT, use it to get a contact normal for two boxes, and clip their faces into a multi-point manifold. Engine: physics/sat.hpp. Demo: chapters/14-sat/main.cpp.

The idea: look for a gap in some shadow

Intuition Shine a light on two shapes and look at their shadows on a wall. If you can find any angle of light where the two shadows have a gap between them, the shapes can’t be touching — that gap is a wall you could slide between them. The Separating-Axis Theorem promises that for convex shapes, if such an angle exists at all, you only need to check a small, fixed set of “candidate” directions to find it.

Try it yourself. Spin the test axis; the bars are the two shapes’ shadows projected onto it. Slide the shapes apart until you can find an axis with a gap:

Which axes to test

For two boxes, the candidate directions are exactly fifteen:

  • the 3 face normals of box A,
  • the 3 face normals of box B,
  • and the 9 cross products of an edge from A with an edge from B (for the awkward edge-on-edge cases).

Project both boxes onto each axis and measure the overlap. If any axis shows a gap, they’re apart — stop immediately. If all fifteen overlap, they’re colliding, and the axis with the smallest overlap is the shallowest way out — that’s the contact normal, and the overlap is the penetration depth.

Projecting a box onto an axis A box’s shadow on a unit axis L reaches out from its centre by h0|a0·L|+h1|a1·L|+h2|a2·L| — each half-extent times how much its axis leans along L. The two shadows overlap when the gap between the centres (measured along L) is less than the sum of those reaches.

From a normal to a manifold

A normal alone isn’t enough. Rest a box on the floor and push it down at a single point — it topples. Real resting contact needs several points spread across the touching area: a manifold. We build one by clipping the two faces that came together.

Face clipping, in plain terms The box that owns the shallowest axis provides the reference face; the other box’s most-facing face is the incident face. We trim the incident face against the sides of the reference face — like using the reference face as a cookie cutter — and keep the trimmed corners that lie below it. Those (up to four) points are the manifold.

Experiments

  • In the widget, set the separation so the shapes just barely overlap, then sweep the axis. Every axis reports overlap — that’s SAT confirming a collision. Now slide them fully apart: somewhere in the sweep a gap appears, and one separating axis is enough to prove they’re clear.
  • In scene 1, count the manifold points as the boxes slide: four when faces are flush, fewer as they slide off the edge. That count is exactly why stacking needs clipping, not a single point.

Takeaways

  • SAT: two convex shapes are apart iff some axis separates their projections. For boxes, 15 axes suffice.
  • The smallest-overlap axis is the contact normal; its overlap is the penetration depth.
  • Clip the touching faces into a manifold of several points — what stable resting contact needs.
