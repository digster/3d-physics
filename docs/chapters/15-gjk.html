Part IV · Collision Detection · Chapter 15

GJK & EPA

SAT was great for boxes, but its axis list is different for every shape. GJK is the general answer: one algorithm that tests any two convex shapes, knowing nothing about them except how to answer one question — “what’s your farthest point in this direction?” It’s one of the most elegant ideas in all of collision detection.

Goal

Understand support functions and the Minkowski difference, how GJK hunts for the origin, and how EPA recovers penetration depth. Engine: physics/gjk.hpp. Demo: chapters/15-gjk/main.cpp.

The only thing GJK needs: a support function

A support function takes a direction and returns the shape’s farthest point that way. For a convex hull it’s trivial — the vertex with the biggest dot product. That single function is all GJK knows about a shape, which is why the same code handles a cube, a sphere, a capsule, or any convex blob.

The clever trick: the Minkowski difference

Intuition Take every point of shape A, subtract every point of shape B, and collect the results into a new shape — the Minkowski difference A⊖B. Here’s the magic: A and B overlap exactly when this new shape contains the origin. Why? If A and B share a point p, then p−p=0 is in the difference. So the whole collision question — “do two shapes overlap?” — becomes one tidy question: “does this one shape contain the origin?”

GJK: hunting the origin with a simplex

GJK looks for the origin inside A⊖B without ever building the whole thing. It keeps a simplex — the simplest shape in each dimension: a point, then a line, a triangle, a tetrahedron — made of support points. Each step it asks “which part of my simplex is closest to the origin?”, throws away the rest, and fetches a new support point in the direction of the origin. Either it wraps a tetrahedron around the origin (they overlap!) or a new support point fails to reach past the origin (there’s a gap — they’re apart).

Why a “simplex”? A simplex is just the minimal shape you need to pin down a region: 2 points make a line segment, 3 a triangle, 4 a tetrahedron. In 3D, a tetrahedron is the smallest thing that can enclose a point — so if GJK ever builds one around the origin, the origin is trapped inside, and the shapes overlap.

EPA: from “yes” to “how much”

GJK only answers yes or no. When it’s yes, the solver still needs a normal and a depth — so the Expanding Polytope Algorithm takes over from GJK’s final tetrahedron. It repeatedly finds the polytope face closest to the origin, pushes a fresh support point outward through it, and re-stitches the surface — growing the polytope until it hugs the boundary of the Minkowski difference. The closest face at the end gives the penetration normal and depth: the shortest shove that separates the shapes.

A real bug we hit EPA is fiddly. Our first version diverged into thousands of faces because the starting tetrahedron’s triangles weren’t consistently wound — a shared edge appeared the same direction on two faces, so the “which faces can the new point see?” bookkeeping fell apart. The fix (a consistently-wound face set, and flipping only the normal vector rather than the vertex order) is the kind of detail that separates a working EPA from an infinite loop. See physics/gjk.cpp and LEARNINGS.md.

Run it

./build/bin/ch15_gjk            # 1 cube+octahedron · 2 two octahedra · 3 tetra+cube

One shape drifts through another. GJK decides overlap; when they intersect, both turn coral and EPA draws the green push-out vector (normal × depth). Notice the code never special-cases “cube” or “octahedron” — it only ever calls the support function.

Experiments

  • Compare with Chapter 14: GJK/EPA gets the same normal and depth on boxes that SAT does (the unit tests check this), but it also handles the octahedron and tetrahedron that SAT had no axis list for.
  • Pause right as the shapes separate and watch the HUD flip from “OVERLAPPING” to “separate” — that’s GJK failing to trap the origin.

Takeaways

  • GJK needs only a support function, so it works for any convex shape.
  • Two shapes overlap ⟺ their Minkowski difference contains the origin.
  • GJK hunts the origin with a growing simplex; EPA then expands a polytope to recover the penetration normal and depth.
End of Part IV The engine can now find every contact — near-pairs, exact points, normals, and depths, for spheres, boxes, and arbitrary convex shapes. But detected contacts still do nothing; bodies interpenetrate and freeze. Part V is the payoff: the impulse solver that turns a contact into a real bounce, makes friction grip, and lets a pile of boxes stack and fall asleep.
