Rotation & Quaternions

Position was easy — three numbers. Orientation is famously not. The obvious choice, three angles, has a trap called gimbal lock that has crashed real aircraft and spacecraft. Quaternions are the fix: four numbers that rotate smoothly, never jam, and don’t drift. Let’s see why we need them and how they work.

Goal

Understand gimbal lock, then quaternions — how they store a rotation, rotate a vector, compose, and update from an angular velocity. Code: common/math/quat.hpp; demo: chapters/10-quaternions/main.cpp.

The trap: Euler angles and gimbal lock

The intuitive way to describe an orientation is three angles: yaw (turn left/right), pitch (nose up/down), and roll (tilt). Apply them one after another and you can reach any orientation. So what goes wrong?

Gimbal lock When pitch reaches 90° — nose straight up — the yaw axis and the roll axis line up and point the same way. Now turning the yaw knob and turning the roll knob do the exact same thing. You’ve lost a degree of freedom: there’s a direction you simply cannot rotate anymore without first pitching back down. That’s gimbal lock, and no amount of cleverness with three angles removes it — it’s built into the representation.

A better mental model: axis + angle

Here’s a fact that untangles everything (Euler’s rotation theorem): any orientation, however complicated, is a single rotation by some angle about some single axis. Not three sequential turns — one. That “axis and angle” picture is the honest description of a rotation, and it’s exactly what a quaternion encodes.

Quaternions: four numbers, no lock

A quaternion stores that axis-and-angle rotation as four numbers. For a rotation of angle θ about a unit axis a:

q=( cosθ2, a sinθ2 )
Why the half-angle? The θ/2 looks bizarre at first. It comes from how a quaternion rotates a vector: it multiplies from both sides, qvq−1 (a “sandwich”). Applying the rotation twice-over like that doubles the angle, so to end up rotating by θ you store θ/2. You almost never build one by hand — you call Quat::fromAxisAngle(axis, angle) and let it do the trig.

The four numbers are stored (w, x, y, z) with w the scalar part, matching the notation q=w+xi+yj+zk. A unit quaternion (length 1) represents a pure rotation; we normalize (rescale to length 1) after operations that might nudge it off, which is far cheaper than the equivalent fix for a matrix.

The three things we do with them

Rotate a vector

Apply the rotation with the sandwich product v′=qvq−1. Our rotate() uses the standard optimised form (two cross products) but the meaning is identical.

Compose rotations

Multiplying two quaternions chains their rotations, just like matrices — and, just like matrices, order matters and reads right-to-left: ab means “do b, then a.” This is the non-commutative heart of 3D rotation.

Spin over time

Given an angular velocity ω (its direction is the spin axis, its length the spin rate), the orientation changes by

dqdt =12 ω⊗q

— the rotational cousin of “position += velocity · dt”. This is the line that makes a tumbling body actually turn, and it’s the bridge into Chapter 11.

Interpolate smoothly (slerp)

Slerp — “spherical linear interpolation” — blends between two orientations along the shortest arc at a constant rate. It’s the right way to ease a camera from one pose to another, and something you simply cannot do by averaging matrices or angles.

Run it

./build/bin/ch10_quaternions    # 1 Euler/gimbal lock · 2 quaternion spin · 3 slerp

Scene 1 drives a little ship with Euler angles while pitch sweeps toward ±90°; watch the red roll axis swing up to meet the green yaw axis, and the HUD announce the lock. Scene 2 spins the same ship with a quaternion — it tumbles through every orientation forever without a hitch. Scene 3 slerps between two poses.

Experiments

  • In scene 1, pause (Space) right when the HUD says GIMBAL LOCK and orbit around: the red and green axes are the same line. That’s the lost degree of freedom, frozen for you to inspect.
  • Let scene 2 run for a long time. A matrix-based version would slowly shear from accumulated rounding error; the renormalised quaternion stays a perfect rotation.

Takeaways

  • Three Euler angles suffer gimbal lock: at pitch 90° two axes collapse and you lose a degree of freedom.
  • Any rotation is really one turn about one axis — which a quaternion stores as four numbers (the half-angle falls out of the sandwich product).
  • Quaternions rotate vectors, compose (right-to-left), integrate from ω, and slerp — all without locking or drifting.
