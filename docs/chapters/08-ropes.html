Ropes & Constraints

A rope shouldn’t stretch. You could make one from very stiff springs, but stiff springs fight the integrator and explode. This chapter takes a completely different, wonderfully stable route: let the beads move freely, then just move them back to the right distance. That single idea — a position constraint — carries us all the way to cloth.

Goal

Learn Verlet integration and Jakobsen’s constraint projection, and use them to build a rope that can’t explode. New engine file: physics/constraint.hpp (plus the Verlet path in physics/particle.hpp). Demo: chapters/08-ropes/main.cpp.

Why not just use stiff springs?

The trap A rope made of springs must be very stiff to look inextensible. But (as Chapter 7 warned) a stiff spring reacts violently: within one timestep it can overshoot so hard that the next step is worse, and the rope shakes itself to infinity. You can fight this with a tiny timestep, but that’s slow. There’s a better way.

Verlet integration: motion without a velocity variable

The position-based approach starts by changing how we integrate. Verlet integration doesn’t store a velocity at all. Instead it remembers where the particle was last step, and reads the velocity from the gap between then and now:

xnext=x+ (x−xprev) +a·Δt2
Intuition The term (x−xprev) is “how far it moved last step” — that is its velocity (times Δt), so the particle keeps coasting the same way (inertia), then acceleration bends the path. The magic is what happens when we then directly edit a particle’s position: because velocity is implied by the position history, moving a particle to fix a constraint automatically updates its velocity for free. That’s what makes constraints and Verlet a perfect match.

A distance constraint: just move them back

A rope segment says “these two beads must be exactly L apart.” After integrating freely, they usually aren’t — so we compute how far off they are and slide them along the line between them until they are. The move is split between the two beads in proportion to their inverse mass, so a light bead moves a lot and a heavy (or pinned, invMass=0) one barely moves:

Vec3 delta = pb.position - pa.position;      // from a to b
const Real dist = length(delta);
const Real diff = (dist - restLength) / dist;    // fractional error
const Vec3  correction = delta * (stiffness * diff / (pa.invMass + pb.invMass));
pa.position += correction * pa.invMass;      // each moves in proportion to 1/mass
pb.position -= correction * pb.invMass;

Why we iterate

Here’s the catch: fixing bead 2–3 can pull bead 2 away from where bead 1–2 wanted it. Satisfying one constraint can break its neighbour. The fix is delightfully simple: do it again. Sweep the whole rope several times per step and the positions relax toward satisfying everything at once. More iterations ⇒ a stiffer, less stretchy rope.

The per-step loop is just those two ideas back to back:

for (Particle& p : particles) p.integrateVerlet(gravity, dt);  // 1. move freely
satisfyConstraints(constraints, particles, iterations);        // 2. pull back N times
Pinning is free A rope has to hang from something. We pin a bead by giving it invMass = 0: Verlet skips it (it never moves) and the constraint solver puts all of a link’s correction onto the other, movable bead. No special cases — the same inverse-mass math from Chapter 6 does the work.

Experiments

  • Press [ repeatedly to drop the iteration count toward 1. The rope turns visibly stretchy and rubbery. Press ] to crank it back up and it stiffens toward inextensible. You’re watching convergence happen.
  • Compare with Chapter 7’s spring chain (scene 3 there). Same swinging motion, but this rope holds its length no matter how hard it’s yanked — the whole point.

Takeaways

  • Stiff springs explode; position constraints don’t stretch in the first place.
  • Verlet infers velocity from position history, so editing a position updates its velocity automatically.
  • A distance constraint just slides two particles to the right separation, split by inverse mass.
  • Iterate the whole constraint list to make everything agree; more passes ⇒ stiffer.
