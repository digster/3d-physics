Friction & the Solver

A bounce needs one impulse. A stack needs many, all pulling against each other, plus friction to stop the whole thing squirting sideways. This chapter adds the two ingredients that turn "bodies that bounce" into "bodies that pile up and stay put": Coulomb friction, and iterating the solver.

Goal

Add friction with the Coulomb cone, understand why the solver must iterate, and see what warm starting buys. Engine: physics/solver.hpp. Demo: chapters/17-friction/main.cpp.

Friction: a sideways impulse, up to a limit

Where the normal impulse acts along the contact normal, friction acts across it — in the tangent plane — opposing any sliding. But friction can't be unlimited, or nothing would ever slide. The Coulomb model caps it: the friction impulse can be at most μ times the normal impulse.

|Jt| ≤μ Jn
The friction cone Picture the total contact impulse as an arrow. The normal part points straight out of the surface; friction tilts it sideways. Coulomb's law says the arrow must stay inside a cone around the normal, with steepness set by μ. Grip is free up to the cone's edge; ask for more sideways force than the cone allows and the surfaces slip — the impulse is clamped to the edge. A big μ is a wide, grippy cone (rubber); a small one is a narrow, slippery cone (ice).

Why the solver iterates

Here's the catch with a stack: fixing the top contact changes the box below it, which changes the one below that. One pass can't satisfy them all at once. So we do what Chapter 8's rope did — iterate. Each pass fixes every contact's velocity in turn, and the corrections ripple down the stack until they agree. This is the sequential impulse method (really Gauss–Seidel iteration), and it's why a few cheap passes can hold up a pyramid.

Warm starting: a running head start

Warm starting A resting stack barely changes frame to frame, so the impulses it needed last frame are almost exactly the ones it needs now. Warm starting re-applies last frame's accumulated impulses as the starting guess, so the iteration begins already near the answer instead of from zero. The difference is dramatic: a warm-started stack is rock-solid at a handful of iterations; without it, the same stack visibly sags and jitters. The demo lets you toggle it (key F) to feel it.

The friction solve, clamped to the cone, looks like this (per tangent direction):

const Real maxFriction = friction * normalImpulse;     // the cone's current edge
const Real vt = dot(A.velocityAtPoint(p) - B.velocityAtPoint(p), t);
Real dLambda = -vt * tMass;
const Real old = tangentImpulse;
tangentImpulse = clamp(old + dLambda, -maxFriction, maxFriction);   // stay in the cone
dLambda = tangentImpulse - old;
A.applyImpulse(t * dLambda, p);
B.applyImpulse(-t * dLambda, p);
Position correction, gently Solving velocities stops bodies approaching, but they may already overlap. We nudge them apart with a little Baumgarte bias — a small separating velocity proportional to how deep the overlap is, beyond a tolerance ("slop"). Too much and stacks bounce and gain energy; we take the max of the restitution and Baumgarte biases (not their sum) so position correction never inflates a real bounce.

Run it

./build/bin/ch17_friction       # 1 tower · 2 pyramid · 3 friction ramp
                                #  [ / ] iterations,  F toggles warm starting

Scene 3 sits three boxes of different friction on a slope: the slippery one (coral) slides off, the grippy one (teal) holds. Scenes 1 and 2 are a tower and a pyramid — the payoff of iteration and warm starting.

Experiments

  • Press [ to drop the solver to 1–2 iterations. The tower turns to jelly. Press ] to raise it and watch the stack firm up — you're literally tuning how well the constraints get satisfied.
  • Press F to turn warm starting off. Even at high iterations the stack softens and sinks; turn it back on and it snaps rigid. That's a great initial guess doing the solver's job for it.

Takeaways

  • Friction is a tangent impulse capped at μJn — the Coulomb cone; inside it grips, on it slips.
  • Stacks need the solver to iterate (sequential impulse) so corrections propagate.
  • Warm starting re-uses last frame's impulses and is what makes stacks stable cheaply.
