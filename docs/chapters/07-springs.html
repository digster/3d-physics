Springs & Dampers

Connect two particles with a spring and you can build soft, springy structures — but a pure spring rings forever. Add a damper and the motion settles. How it settles — overshooting, or gliding smoothly to rest — is governed by one number, and it’s worth understanding deeply because damping shows up everywhere from suspension to UI animation.

Goal

Learn Hooke’s law, add damping, and understand the three damping regimes (under, critical, over). New force generators live in physics/forces.hpp; the demo is chapters/07-springs/main.cpp.

Hooke’s law: the spring force

A spring wants to be a particular length, its rest length. Stretch it or squash it and it pushes back, harder the further you are from rest:

F=−k·x

Here x is the stretch (current length minus rest length) and k is the stiffness — a big k is a stiff spring. The minus sign is the whole personality of a spring: the force always points back toward rest. Stretch it and it pulls in; squash it and it pushes out.

Why a plain spring never stops

Intuition A spring endlessly trades potential energy (stored in the stretch) for kinetic energy (motion) and back. Pull a mass down and release: the spring yanks it up, it overshoots rest, compresses, gets pushed back down, overshoots again… With nothing to remove energy, it oscillates forever. To make it settle we need something that drains energy — and that something is a damper.

The damper: resist the speed of stretching

A damper is like a shock absorber: a force proportional to how fast the spring is currently lengthening or shortening, opposing that change. If c is the damping coefficient and vrel is the rate of stretch (the relative velocity of the ends along the spring), the damper adds −cvrel. It always fights motion, so it can only remove energy — exactly what we want.

The three regimes

Combine spring and damper and the settling behaviour falls into three cases, decided by the damping ratio ζ (“zeta”), which compares your damping to the special “critical” amount ccrit=2km:

  • Under-damped (ζ<1): overshoots the target and rings before settling. Think a plucked guitar string.
  • Critically damped (ζ=1): returns to rest as fast as possible without overshooting. The sweet spot for a door closer or a camera that should snap to a target.
  • Over-damped (ζ>1): returns slowly, as if moving through honey — no overshoot, but sluggish.

The code

Both effects live in one function: the spring term plus the damping term, applied equal-and-opposite to the two ends (so the pair’s momentum is untouched):

Vec3 delta = pa - pb;                       // vector between the two ends
const Real dist = length(delta);
const Vec3 dir  = delta * (1 / dist);       // unit direction along the spring

const Real stretch = dist - rest;
Real magnitude = -k * stretch;              // Hooke: pull back toward rest

if (c != 0) {                               // damping: resist the rate of stretch
    const Real closingSpeed = dot(va - vb, dir);
    magnitude -= c * closingSpeed;
}
return dir * magnitude;                      // apply +F to a, -F to b
A subtlety: stiff springs and the timestep

The stiffer the spring (big k), the faster it reacts — and the smaller a timestep the integrator needs to keep up. Push k too high for your Δt and even semi-implicit Euler will buzz or blow up. That tension is exactly why the next chapter reaches for a completely different, unconditionally stable technique for things that must be rigid, like ropes.

Experiments

  • In the widget, set ζ to exactly 1.00 and nudge it either way. Just below, a tiny overshoot appears; just above, it slows down. Critical damping really is the knife-edge fastest-without-overshoot.
  • Scene 3 is a chain of springs — a soft pendulum. Notice it both swings and stretches. That stretchiness is the spring approach’s weakness, and the motivation for Chapter 8.

Takeaways

  • Hooke’s law: F=−kx, a restoring force proportional to stretch.
  • A pure spring conserves energy and rings forever; a damper removes energy by resisting the rate of stretch.
  • The damping ratio ζ sets the regime: under (rings), critical (fastest, no overshoot), over (sluggish).
  • Stiff springs demand small timesteps — the opening for position-based methods.
