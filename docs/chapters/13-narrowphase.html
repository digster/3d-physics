Part IV · Collision Detection · Chapter 13

Narrowphase: Spheres & Planes

Broadphase said “these two might touch.” Now we answer exactly: do they, and if so, where, which way, and how deep? Those three answers — a point, a normal, and a penetration depth — form a contact, the single most important data structure in a physics engine.

Goal

Define the contact, and generate one for the easy shapes: sphere–sphere, sphere–plane, and sphere–box. Engine: physics/contact.hpp, physics/narrowphase.hpp. Demo: chapters/13-narrowphase/main.cpp.

The contact

Every narrowphase test produces the same three things:

  • the contact point — roughly where they touch,
  • the contact normal — a unit vector saying which direction to push to separate them,
  • the penetration depth — how far they currently overlap.
A convention worth fixing once Sign bugs in collision code are miserable to track down, so we pick one rule and never break it: the normal points from B toward A — the direction you’d shove body A to pull the pair apart. Every test in this part obeys it, and the Part V solver can then trust it blindly.

Sphere vs sphere

The friendliest case in all of collision detection. Two spheres touch when the distance between their centres is less than the sum of their radii:

|cA−cB| <rA+rB

The normal is just the direction between the centres; the penetration is however much the radii overlap. Three lines of code, and you can already build a ball pit.

Sphere vs plane

A plane is {x:n·x=d} — every point whose dot with the unit normal n equals the offset d. The signed distance of the sphere’s centre from the plane is n·c−d; if that’s less than the radius, they touch, and the normal is simply the plane’s normal. This one test, pointed inward on five planes, is all it takes to build a box that catches falling balls.

Sphere vs box

The trick: find the closest point on the box to the sphere’s centre by clamping the centre (in the box’s local frame) to the box’s extents. If that closest point is within a radius, they touch. (If the centre is inside the box, we push it out through the nearest face instead.) The same clamp-to-the-box idea reappears constantly in collision code.

Run it

./build/bin/ch13_narrowphase    # 1 box · 2 more balls · 3 + a static obstacle

Balls rain into an open container. Broadphase (Chapter 12) narrows the sphere–sphere pairs; the tests above turn survivors into contacts, drawn as a coral dot at the point and an amber arrow along the normal.

This is detection, not response To keep the pile watchable the demo does the crudest possible fix — shove overlapping balls apart and cancel the velocity heading into the surface. That is not a real solver: there’s no bounciness, no friction, no stable stacking, and no spin. Making contacts push back properly — with restitution, friction, and rotation — is the whole of Part V. Here, the point is simply that we can find the contacts.

Experiments

  • Orbit under the pile and watch the amber normals: on the floor they point straight up; between two balls they point along the line joining the centres. The normal always says “this way out.”
  • Scene 3 adds a big static sphere. Balls detect contact against it via the same sphere–sphere test — an immovable body is just one with infinite mass.

Takeaways

  • A contact is a point + a normal + a penetration depth — the output of all narrowphase, the input to all response.
  • Fix the normal convention once (here, B → A) and never break it.
  • Sphere tests are cheap: distance-vs-radii, signed distance to a plane, or clamp-to-box.
