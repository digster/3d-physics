Particles & Forces

Time to build actual physics. A particle is the simplest thing a force can act on — a point with a mass. String a few hundred together and you have a fountain. The pattern we set up here, the “force accumulator”, is the backbone of every force-based engine in the rest of the course.

Goal

Understand Newton’s second law as code, meet the force-accumulator loop, and use it to build a fountain driven by gravity and air drag. New engine files: physics/particle.hpp and physics/forces.hpp. Demo: chapters/06-particles/main.cpp.

What is a particle?

A particle is a point mass: it has a position and a velocity and a mass, but no size and no orientation. That’s a feature, not a limitation — with nothing to rotate, the physics is pure Newton and we can focus entirely on how forces move things. (Rotation arrives in Part III, and it’s a whole story of its own.)

Newton’s second law, as code

The one law this chapter rests on:

F=ma

Force equals mass times acceleration. We know the forces and the mass; we want the motion, so we rearrange to a=F/m. That acceleration feeds the semi-implicit Euler integrator from Chapter 5: velocity changes by a·Δt, then position changes by the new velocity.

Why we store inverse mass

Look closely and Particle stores invMass (that is, 1/m) rather than mass. Two reasons. First, a=F/m becomes a multiply (F·invMass), which is both faster and tidier. Second — and this is the big one — it gives us a clean way to say “infinitely heavy, never moves”: an inverse mass of 0. Anchors, pinned rope ends, and pinned cloth corners are all just particles with invMass = 0. You’ll see that trick constantly from here on.

The force-accumulator pattern

Real objects feel several forces at once — gravity and drag and wind. The elegant way to handle that is the superposition principle: forces simply add. So each step we zero an accumulator, let every force add its contribution, then integrate once with the total.

p.clearForces();
force::gravity(p, {0, -9.81f, 0});   // each generator only ADDS to forceAccum
if (useDrag) force::drag(p, 0.06f, 0.02f);
p.integrateForces(dt);               // a = forceAccum * invMass, then Euler

Gravity treats every mass the same

Gravity is special: heavy and light things fall at the same rate (Galileo’s leaning-tower point). Our gravity generator gets this right by adding a force of mg — so when the integrator divides by m, the mass cancels and the acceleration is exactly g:

void gravity(Particle& p, const Vec3& g) {
    if (p.isStatic()) return;
    p.addForce(g * p.mass());        // F = m·g  →  a = F/m = g, for any mass
}

Drag: air fights back

Without drag, a fountain droplet would trace a perfect parabola and bounce forever. Drag is a force that always opposes motion, growing with speed. We use the standard two-term model — a linear part and a squared part:

Fdrag=−v^(k1|v|+k2|v|2)

Here v^ (“v-hat”) is the unit vector in the direction of motion, so the whole force points backwards along the velocity. The k1 term dominates when slow, the k2 term when fast. Drag is what makes the droplets arc believably and lose energy on each bounce until they settle.

Making a fountain

The demo keeps a fixed pool of particles. Each gets launched from the nozzle with a randomised upward velocity and a lifetime; when its life runs out (or it settles on the floor) it is recycled — teleported back to the nozzle with a fresh launch. That recycling is why a couple hundred particles can look like an endless spray. Colour is mapped to speed, so you can literally see the fast upward jet (amber) slow, arc over, and fall (blue).

Run it

./build/bin/ch06_particles      # 1 fountain · 2 + wind · 3 no drag

Experiments

  • Press 3 to turn drag off and compare the arcs with scene 1. Without drag the droplets fly higher and keep their energy, bouncing longer.
  • Press 2 to add a steady wind — a constant sideways force added to the accumulator. Notice how it just adds to gravity; that’s superposition.
  • In the code, change the launch speed range in respawn and watch the fountain’s height respond.

Takeaways

  • A particle is a point mass — position, velocity, mass, no rotation.
  • a=F/m; storing inverse mass makes this a multiply and lets 0 mean “immovable”.
  • The force-accumulator pattern: clear → add every force → integrate once. Forces superpose, so order is irrelevant.
  • Gravity adds mg so all masses fall equally; drag opposes velocity.
