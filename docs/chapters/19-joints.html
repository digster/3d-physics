Part VI · Constraints & Joints · Chapter 19

Joints

Contacts keep bodies apart. Joints hold them together — a chain link to the next, a door to its frame, an arm to a shoulder. And here's the lovely part: a joint is just another constraint the impulse solver already knows how to satisfy. Same machinery, one rule relaxed.

Goal

Understand joints as two-sided constraints, and build the three that cover most needs: distance, ball-socket, and hinge. Engine: physics/joint.hpp. Demo: chapters/19-joints/main.cpp.

One-sided vs two-sided

Intuition A contact is a one-sided constraint: a floor can push a ball up but never pull it down, so its impulse is clamped to be non-negative. A joint is two-sided: a rod between two beads keeps them exactly a fixed distance apart, shortening AND lengthening as needed — so its impulse is unclamped. That single difference — drop the clamp — turns the contact solver into a joint solver. Everything else (effective mass, warm starting, a Baumgarte nudge for drift) is identical.

The general recipe

Every joint follows the same three steps the contact solver did:

  1. Write the thing that should be true as a constraint C=0 (e.g. "these two points coincide").
  2. Its time-derivative gives a velocity constraint C˙=0 ("the two points have no relative velocity").
  3. Apply an impulse that drives C˙ to zero, plus a small bias to erase any leftover position error.

Distance joint — a rigid rod

The simplest joint keeps two anchor points a fixed distance apart. It's a scalar constraint along the line between them — identical to a contact's normal impulse, but unclamped (a rod can pull as well as push) and targeting a specific length. A chain of these is a rope that cannot stretch.

Ball-socket joint — pin two points together

A ball-socket (or point-to-point) joint forces two anchor points to coincide, leaving the bodies free to rotate about that shared point — a shoulder, a hip, a chain link. Now the constraint is a 3D vector (three equations at once), so the effective "mass" is a 3×3 matrix. We build it from the masses and inertias and invert it:

K= (mA−1 +mB−1)E −[rA]IA−1[rA] −[rB]IB−1[rB]

where [r] is the skew (cross-product) matrix of the lever arm. The impulse to fix the joint each pass is P=K−1(−C˙−bias) — a 3×3 solve, applied equal-and-opposite to the two bodies.

Hinge joint — a shared axis

A hinge is a ball-socket that also forces the two bodies to share one spin axis, so the only freedom left is rotation about that line — a door, an elbow, a knee. On top of the point constraint we add a small angular constraint that cancels any relative spin perpendicular to the axis (two more equations), keeping the axes aligned while leaving the swing free.

A bug worth its own paragraph Our first hinge went berserk — the door's angular velocity spiked into the thousands and it locked up horizontal. The cause wasn't the joint math at all: the door's collider and the frame's collider overlapped at the pivot, so the contact solver kept shoving them apart, fighting the joint that held them together. The fix is what every engine does: jointed bodies don't collide with each other. With that one exemption the hinge swung perfectly. (Also, two ball-sockets do NOT make a good hinge — that's six equations for a five-constraint joint, so they fight; the dedicated 3+2 formulation is the right one.)

Run it

./build/bin/ch19_joints         # 1 hanging chain · 2 hinged trapdoor · 3 ragdoll

Scene 1 is a chain of ball-socket links released from horizontal — watch it whip down and settle into a hanging curve. Scene 2 is a trapdoor on a hinge with a box riding it, which swings open and dumps the box. Scene 3 is a ragdoll — a torso, head, arms, and legs joined by ball sockets — dropped in a heap.

Experiments

  • In scene 1, count the links and note the chain never stretches, however hard it whips — that's the unclamped constraint holding each link's spacing exactly.
  • Scene 3: the ragdoll is nothing but boxes and ball sockets, yet it collapses like a body. Complex behaviour, simple parts — the recurring theme of the whole course.

Takeaways

  • A joint is a two-sided constraint: same impulse solver as a contact, without the "≥ 0" clamp.
  • Distance fixes a length; ball-socket pins a point (a 3×3 effective mass); hinge adds an axis-alignment constraint.
  • Jointed bodies must be exempt from colliding with each other, or contacts fight the joint.
