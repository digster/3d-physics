SDL3 · C++ · No engine, no magic

Learn 3D physics by building it from scratch.

A chapter-by-chapter course that takes you from “what is a vector” to a small, real rigid-body engine with collisions, friction, joints, and soft bodies. Every idea comes as a runnable demo and a page like this that explains the intuition, the math, and the code.

Who this is for

You should be comfortable reading C++. Beyond that, no prior physics, graphics, or advanced math is assumed — we reintroduce every idea (vectors, matrices, a bit of calculus intuition) as we need it, and gloss the jargon the first time it appears. Read the chapters in order; each one builds directly on the last. If a page ever feels like it skipped a step, that’s a bug in the writing — the goal is that a motivated beginner can follow every line.

Start here — Part I: Foundations

These five chapters build the toolkit the rest of the course stands on: an SDL3 loop, the vector/matrix/quaternion math, a from-scratch software 3D renderer, and the numerical integration that moves a simulation forward in time.

Euler, symplectic Euler, and RK4 raced head-to-head — why the timestep is the soul of a stable sim.

Part II: Particles & Forces

Now we build the physics. These four chapters introduce the from-scratch engine (physics/) and the two great families of simulation: force-based (accumulate forces, then integrate) and position-based (move freely, then satisfy constraints). By the end you have cloth rippling in the wind.

Chapter 6
Particles & Forces
Newton’s F = m·a and the force-accumulator pattern, shown as a fountain of hundreds of droplets.
Chapter 7
Springs & Dampers
Hooke’s law and damping — under, critical, and over — with an interactive settling-curve widget.
Chapter 8
Ropes & Constraints
Verlet integration and Jakobsen position projection: a rope that can’t explode, however you yank it.
Chapter 9
Cloth
A grid of constraints — structural, shear, and bend — woven into a flag that waves in the wind.

Part III: Rigid Bodies

Everything so far has been a point. Now things get an orientation and spin — the rotational half of Newton’s laws. Rotation turns out to be surprisingly deep: gimbal lock, quaternions, the inertia tensor, and a tumbling handle that flips itself over for no apparent reason.

Chapter 10
Rotation & Quaternions
Why Euler angles jam (gimbal lock), and how quaternions give smooth, drift-free orientation.
Chapter 11
Rigid-Body Dynamics
Torque, the inertia tensor, and the eerie Dzhanibekov flip — a body that tumbles itself over.

Part IV: Collision Detection

Bodies can move and spin, but they still pass through each other. This part teaches the engine to find contacts — a funnel from cheap-and-approximate to exact: throw away far-apart pairs, then compute the precise contact point, normal, and depth. (Making them push back is Part V.)

Chapter 12
Broadphase
Turn the O(N²) all-pairs problem into near-O(N) with AABBs, a sweep, and a spatial grid.
Chapter 13
Narrowphase: Spheres & Planes
Turn a candidate pair into an exact contact — point, normal, and penetration depth.
Chapter 14
SAT & Manifolds
Collide oriented boxes with the Separating-Axis Theorem; clip faces into a multi-point manifold.
Chapter 15
GJK & EPA
One algorithm for any convex shape, via support functions and the Minkowski difference.

Getting the code running

You need a C++20 compiler and CMake. SDL3 is optional — if it isn’t installed, the build fetches and compiles it for you.

# macOS: brew install sdl3 cmake      (Linux: use your package manager)
cmake -B build
cmake --build build -j

# run any chapter (binaries land in build/bin):
./build/bin/ch01_hello_sdl3
./build/bin/ch05_integration

# run the unit tests:
ctest --test-dir build --output-on-failure
Standard controls Every 3D demo shares them: drag orbit · wheel zoom · Space pause · . single-step · R reset · 1–9 scene variant · W wireframe · H HUD · Esc quit.

How the project fits together

Three layers: each chapter’s tiny main.cpp sets up a scene and leans on a shared framework (common/ — the math, the renderer, the loop) and, from Part II on, a from-scratch engine (physics/). The reasoning behind the structure is in ARCHITECTURE.md.

The philosophy No Bullet, no Jolt, no PhysX — and no graphics engine either. We write the integrators, the collision detection, the solver, and even the 3D renderer ourselves, because the point is to understand why each one works.
